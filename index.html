Why We Must Talk About Violence in Mexico
Understanding the violence that grips Mexico today is essential to grasp the daily reality faced by millions. Fear, uncertainty, and the struggle to survive in territories governed by informal networks of power—operating outside the law—have become part of everyday life. These structures, constantly evolving, inflict harm on countless innocent people, whether through forced disappearances or the endless search for missing loved ones. This violence is not part of our identity; it does not define who we are as a nation. And that is precisely why we must talk about it.

The origins of drug trafficking emerge with marijuana and poppy cultivation in the Golden Triangle (Sinaloa, Chihuahua, Durango).

1930s - 40s

The PRI (Institutional Revolutionary Party) claims to bring post-revolutionary stability, but in reality, it centralizes power and builds a system of authoritarian control masked as democracy—laying the groundwork for decades of impunity.

Drug trafficking becomes a tool for territorial control. During Mexico’s Dirty War, the state represses social movements and tolerates certain drug networks.

1970s

The government presents a narrative of national security and development, while secretly waging a brutal internal war against dissidents. During this period, it turns a blind eye to growing drug routes, viewing them as manageable in exchange for territorial control.

The Guadalajara Cartel consolidates power. The 1985 assassination of DEA agent Kiki Camarena marks a turning point in U.S.-Mexico relations.

1980s

While publicly aligning with U.S. anti-narcotics campaigns, the Mexican state protects powerful figures within the Guadalajara Cartel. Anti-corruption efforts are superficial, reinforcing a façade of cooperation without real accountability.

The fall of the Guadalajara Cartel leads to fragmentation and the rise of new cartels: Tijuana, Juárez, and Sinaloa.

1990s

Political transition and democratization are celebrated, yet these reforms fail to dismantle entrenched networks of corruption. The weakening of the PRI reduces centralized control, but also leaves a vacuum exploited by criminal organizations.

President Calderón launches the 'war on drugs.' Militarization fuels extreme violence and the rise of brutal groups like Los Zetas.

2006

President Calderón militarizes public security, framing it as a decisive move against cartels. However, this “war on drugs” lacks social investment and leads to widespread human rights violations—while the root causes of violence remain unaddressed.

Mass graves, forced disappearances, and tragedies like the Ayotzinapa case define the decade. Violence becomes widespread and normalized.

2010

The state fails to guarantee justice and safety. Institutions are overwhelmed and impunity reigns.

Government discourse focuses on institutional reforms and justice initiatives, but these efforts are largely performative. Cases like Ayotzinapa reveal systemic failures and the unwillingness of the state to confront deep-rooted collusion.

2018–2025

Under AMLO, the 'hugs, not bullets' policy claims to seek peace without confrontation. In practice, it avoids tackling organized crime structurally, enabling criminal groups to expand through economic and social infiltration. The state confuses inaction with nonviolence.
